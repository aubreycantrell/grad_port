Spam!

Student Civic Engagement

A mobile app prototype to make on-campus civic life visible and actionable.
HCI · UX Design · 2024

Purpose

Over seven weeks in Human-Computer Interaction (CS220), our team of four designed Spam!, a mobile web prototype to increase student civic participation and organizational transparency at Wellesley College. Modeled after the school’s iconic “spam boards,” the app reimagines physical flyers as a centralized, accessible, and interactive digital platform for civic engagement — making it easier to vote, discover events, and stay informed.

What It Does

Spam! serves as a civic engagement hub for college students — consolidating elections, events, and volunteering opportunities into a clean mobile interface. Students can:

  • Browse upcoming activism and community events
  • Vote securely in organizational elections
  • RSVP, track attendance, and earn badges for participation
  • Access civic resources like voter registration and educational materials

The app fosters both transparency (through visible election results and activity tracking) and motivation (through gamified engagement badges).

What the App Addresses

Interviews revealed that most students rely on posters, word of mouth, and social media for event awareness — channels that are fragmented and easy to forget. Physical spam boards promote visibility but not follow-through. Many students reported missing events simply because they forgot details after walking by a poster. Spam! transforms that analog discovery process into a persistent, digital, and personalized experience that still retains the energy of campus life.

Central Question

How might we increase civic participation and transparency in student life by transforming campus “spam culture” into a digital, interactive system?

Objectives

  • Improve visibility of civic events and organizational elections
  • Enable low-friction voting and event participation
  • Support accountability through visible confirmation and results
  • Encourage consistent engagement through motivational badges

Research

Research Goals

  • Understand how students currently discover and remember campus events
  • Identify barriers to participation and organizational transparency
  • Determine effective motivational and filtering features for engagement apps

Needfinding & Interviews

We conducted contextual interviews with students, faculty, and alumni. Across users, three consistent themes emerged:

  • Time pressure and forgetfulness: Students attend only a small fraction of events they hear about (often <30%).
  • Over-saturation of communication: Users described email lists and physical spam as “cluttered,” desiring relevance and personalization.
  • Desire for streamlined discovery: Students wanted to browse by type (talks, service, protests) or incentives (free food, leadership credit).

Personas & Flows

Wendy — Event Searching

Social connection Timeboxed Filter-first

Snapshot. Friday was blocked for a hangout, but plans fell through. Wendy still wants a social, low-effort plan that fits her saved time slot.

Goal. Find a fun, non-academic event (ideally with free food) happening Friday evening.

Flow in Spam!.

  • Opens Spam! → Filters events (excludes Career/Class orgs; includes Free Food).
  • Returns to the personalized feed with Friday-only results.
  • Finds Wushu Open Practice + Free Boba → opens detail → RSVPs.

Emily — Org Follow-Up

First-year Exploration Asks & Contact

Snapshot. After sampling many events, Emily is deciding which orgs to join. She recently tried BOW Climbing and wants more details.

Goal. Get questions answered and contact someone she met at the event.

Flow in Spam!.

  • Searches for BOW Climbing → opens org page.
  • Skims the User-contributed FAQ; posts a new question when she can’t find one answer.
  • Opens Contact → finds the member she met → sends a friendly note expressing interest.

Sofia — E-board Inquiry, SOI Upload, Voting

Two-year member Leadership Accountability

Snapshot. Long-time member of Counterpoint magazine; wants to step into leadership next year.

Goal. Discover open roles, submit an SOI, and vote with confidence.

Flow in Spam!.

  • Sees elections announcement in feed → taps to Leadership page.
  • Reviews open roles & timelines → uploads SOI (visible alongside other candidates).
  • When polls open, navigates to Voting → casts votes for each role → later checks posted results.

Task & Domain Analysis

  • Find and RSVP to civic events filtered by interest or timing
  • Contact orgs for leadership or follow-up questions
  • Vote in campus or org elections with transparent confirmation

Existing systems like WeEngage, 25Live, and Google Calendar were siloed. Spam! merges them into one cohesive platform.

Design Guidelines

  • Accessibility: High contrast, low-cognitive-load layouts
  • Energetic clarity: Retain the playfulness of physical spam without the clutter
  • Motivation: Reward engagement without creating noise
  • Transparency: Clear ownership, timelines, and accountability in votes

Branding

The palette reinterprets the Spam can’s blue and yellow — blue (#253A77) for structure and yellow (#E4BF47) for calls to action — balancing trustworthiness and energy.

Our low Low-Fidelity wireframes tested navigation and task flow. Each tab was structured around a core function:

  • Voting — Transparent elections with results pages
  • Resources — Educational materials and registration links
  • Profile — Track badges, RSVPs, and event history

Iterations

  • Simplified landing page with onboarding text
  • Added clearer confirmation for votes and RSVPs
  • Introduced badges for engagement and event attendance
  • Improved hierarchy for mobile readability

Risky Interactions

  • Badge Collection: Rewards engagement with badges like “Green Voter” or “Out and About.”
  • Voting Confirmation: Clear feedback via “Vote Sent” screens; supports multiple concurrent elections securely.

Prototyping & Evaluation

We developed a clickable prototype in Balsamiq and a high-fidelity Figma mockup for final presentation. The layout used modular blocks and generous white space for readability while maintaining the vibrancy of campus life.

Testing

Usability testing included in-class pilots and remote moderated sessions. Participants completed three core tasks:

  • RSVP to an event
  • Cast a vote in a student election
  • Upload a Statement of Intent for leadership

Findings

  • Navigation and filtering were rated intuitive and engaging.
  • Users praised visual clarity and consistent color coding.
  • Some onboarding steps required clearer vote confirmation cues.

Adjustments Made

  • Added “My Results” transparency page
  • Clarified SSO authentication pathway
  • Added notification preferences and privacy disclaimers

Reflections & What’s Next

  • Visibility drives participation: Centralized information increased event engagement.
  • Transparency creates trust: Visible confirmation and results improved voting confidence.
  • Gamification works when balanced: Badges motivated but didn’t distract.
  • Accessibility through simplicity: A limited palette and hierarchy improved cognitive flow.

What’s Next

  • Integrate authentication and secure data infrastructure
  • Allow custom notifications (events, elections)
  • Run longitudinal usability studies to track engagement

Spam! demonstrates how HCI principles—needfinding, prototyping, and iterative testing—can transform everyday campus systems into accessible civic tools that inspire active participation and accountability.
